Examining Technical Challenges of Biomedical Engineering Students During Clinical Training in Ghana

Research Project & Analysis | Department of Biomedical Engineering

Abstract Technical Challenges Professional Impact Recommendations

Abstract

Clinical training is a cornerstone of Biomedical Engineering (BME) education in Ghana, bridging the gap between theoretical knowledge and practical hospital application. However, many students face significant technical hurdles during their rotations in Ghanaian healthcare facilities. This project examines these challenges—ranging from equipment obsolescence to inadequate supervision—and evaluates how these obstacles shape the professional development and competency of future engineers.

Core Technical Challenges

Equipment Obsolescence

Exposure to outdated medical devices that do not align with modern curriculum standards, making it difficult to apply contemporary troubleshooting techniques.

Lack of Spare Parts

Frequent inability to complete repairs due to disrupted supply chains for specialized electronic components and mechanical parts within Ghana.

Limited Hands-on Access

High student-to-equipment ratios in major teaching hospitals like Korle-Bu or Komfo Anokye, leading to "observation-only" learning sessions.

Infrastructural Gaps

Unstable power supply and poor tool-kit availability (multimeters, soldering stations, simulators) in clinical engineering departments.

Impact on Professional Development

The technical challenges faced during training have a direct correlation with the confidence and skill acquisition of the BME student:

  • Skill Gaps: Students may graduate with strong theoretical knowledge but lack the "bench-side" confidence to repair critical life-support equipment.
  • Adaptability: Conversely, the scarcity of resources often fosters "improvised problem-solving," a unique trait of Ghanaian engineers.
  • Employability: The mismatch between hospital technology and academic training can lead to a longer onboarding period in the private sector.

Proposed Recommendations

  1. Industry-Academia Partnerships: Universities should collaborate with private medical distributors to provide students access to modern diagnostic tools.
  2. Simulation Labs: Implementing high-fidelity simulation centers on campus to augment clinical rotations.
  3. Standardized Mentorship: Developing a structured logbook system for hospital engineers to ensure uniform training across various regional hospitals.
